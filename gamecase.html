Game Case
The best way to track your video game collection.
Mobile
Product Design

Game Case is a project I started in 2018 with a developer colleague of mine. The idea was simple - we'd seen the success of apps that help users track their TV-watching habits, and as avid gamers ourselves, thought the same principles could be applied to video game collections.


Over the course of a few short days, we developed a prototype that we could use to test - and hopefully prove - the concept. Whilst my colleague did the development, I was responsible for the user experience, visual design, and branding of the product. I also built an interactive prototype for use to use for early user-acceptance testing.

Early Designs

User Flow

Though not a complex product, I felt it was important to identify a core use-case to begin building the app upon. The most basic version of our app would require a sign-up, some basic data from the user, and a database that can be used to either find new games, or add old ones to your collection.


This simple set of requirements allowed us to quickly put together a minimum viable prototype, which you'll see below.



Early Concepts

The layouts and patterns of the app were initially based closely on other apps I found during competitor analysis.


Popular apps like TVTime or iTV had already established patterns that matched our use case.


I began designing the key screens and flows with these apps in mind.

Design Exploration

Although it's a little premature, I wanted to hit the ground running with a strong, clear aesthetic. This was also driven by our competitor analysis, in which I identified a few apps who had similar functionality to that which we intended to offer.


These competitors lacked the fit and polish of a modern piece of software, however. I wanted GameCase to stand out as a quality solution, and I felt the best way to do this was clean, modern aesthetics.


As gamers ourselves, my partner and I also identified that personalisation was important to our customer segment. For that reason, we built the app's colour scheme to be modular. This meant that we would be able to offer new designs and colour patterns in the future.

Design Pillars

If you've spent any time looking over my previous work, you won't be suprised to find that we landed on a green and purple colour scheme. Thankfully, however, I do have reasons for doing so.


The prevalent aesthetic in gaming, and particularly in games-related software, is high-saturation colours (Usually red or blue) on a black background. Think IGN, or OpenCritic, or Discord.


We wanted to seperate ourselves from the pack, so I picked bright, slightly desaturated colours that still held a futuristic or technology vibe. I kept our app predominantly white, as O felt this would give it a modern feel, and help it stand out.

Assets and Resources

IGDB.com

The app really wouldn't be possible without a comprehensive database of video games to pull from. Thankfully, Internet Game Database offers just such an API.


We built the app from the ground up to use their technology. Even my early UX prototypes use assets and copy from their API, to ensure consistency with production.

Toxin UI Kit

I'd had the Toxin UI kit from Metro sitting in my Figma files for a rainy day, and it had the exact look and feel I wanted for Game Case.


Like any good plan, Toxin didn't survive contact with our final product. However, it laid the foundation for a number of major elements of the UI.

Feather Icon Kit

Like Toxin, I really loved the simplicity of X's Feather icons ever since I'd discovered them, and had been eager to find a project where they'd fit.


The rounded, simple linework of Feather gave it a simple, premium, and futuristic feel. Perfect for GameCase.

Production Design & Iteration

Core Journeys

Our original user flow from the start of the project identified three key tasks: discovery and collection. .


For discovery, we had two possible entrypoints our user might use to find a new game - the home screen, or the search. This gave us an easy target for our MVP.


Of course, once they'd found the game they wanted, they'd want to learn about it. For that reason, the game detail screen also took precedence.


Lastly, the end-goal of all this discovery is to add the game to your collection, or your wishlist, so that came last. Our first major flows were starting to take form.

Dark Mode

Much like looking at your phone in a cinema, we realised during early 'playtests' that the white background wasn't awesome if you're in a dark room - something that, as it turns out, is suprisingly common when playing video games.


Selfishly, we added dark-mode to the app to eliminate this particular issue.

News Feed

The home screen, game pages, and collection flows were core to the experience, so my initial effort was focused there.

Filtering

When our user is searching for a new game, it's important they're only fed options that they have the appropriate hardware and consoles for. For this reason, we developed a complex filtering system.


These filters were available during any search, but we also asked the user during sign-up to tell us what hardware they owned. While they can always edit this information, it gives us a great baseline to start offering relevant games.
